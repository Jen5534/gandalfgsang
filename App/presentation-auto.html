SQ-01  ·  Smart Booking
Right desk, first try — not through trial and error
The Challenge
"Help colleagues find a desk that genuinely matches their needs on the first try. Go beyond equipment filters — think about the signals that make a desk right for the task, the person, or the day."
  • What makes a desk the right one for a given colleague or task?
  • How do we surface the right desks without burying people in choice?
  • Should past bookings inform future suggestions — and how visibly?
Perch's Answer
  • Multi-signal scoring — team proximity, neighbourhood fit, desk equipment, time of day, and day-of-week all feed a single confidence score per desk
  • One great recommendation, not fifty options — the top-scoring desk is shown prominently with a ranked shortlist behind it
  • Booking history as preference — desks you've used before score higher automatically, surfaced as "your usual" without you having to ask
  • Silent preference matching — standing desk, accessibility, dual monitor preferences are set once in your profile and applied every time, invisibly
  • Transparent score — the recommendation shows why: "Teammates nearby · Your usual floor · Window seat"
SQ-02  ·  Know Before You Go
Give colleagues certainty before they travel
The Challenge
"How does someone know — ahead of setting off — that they'll find a suitable desk when they walk in? Surface the signal, not just the seat count."
  • What level of certainty is enough to commit to a journey?
  • How do you signal likely availability per floor, not just the building?
  • What happens when confidence is low — do you suggest alternatives?
Perch's Answer
  • Floor Plan view shows live booking density per floor and neighbourhood — colour-coded green/amber/red — before you've left home
  • Who's In view shows which teammates are booked, which floor they're on, and at what time — making the journey feel purposeful, not speculative
  • Occupancy signal, not just seat count — "Neighbourhood 3 is 80% booked" is more actionable than "7 desks free across the building"
  • Low-confidence prompts — if your preferred neighbourhood is nearly full, Perch prompts you to book now or suggests a comparable alternative
  • Walk-in auto-booking safety net — if you arrive without a booking, proximity detection finds and allocates the best available desk immediately
SQ-03  ·  Environmental Comfort
The environment matters — capture it without form-filling
The Challenge
"Temperature, natural light, noise, airflow, proximity to a window or aircon vent — these quietly shape whether a desk works for someone. How could these influence choice without turning booking into a form-filling exercise?"
  • Which environmental signals are worth capturing — and how?
  • Sensor data, colleague feedback, or both? Who owns the source of truth?
  • How do personal preferences get matched to the space without friction?
Perch's Answer
  • Rich desk metadata — each desk carries attributes: window proximity, quiet/collaborative zone, near aircon, standing desk, AV equipment, accessibility features
  • Set-once preferences — "I prefer window seats and quiet zones" is stored in your profile and applied silently to every booking. Never asked twice.
  • Post-booking micro-feedback — one tap after your session to flag "too cold", "too noisy", or "great spot". This enriches the desk's environmental profile over time.
  • Community signal → admin alert — when multiple users flag the same issue on a desk (e.g. "always cold"), admin receives an automated facilities alert
  • Roadmap: IoT sensor integration — CO₂, temperature, and noise sensors feed live environmental scores into the booking engine in Phase 3
SQ-04  ·  Plans Change
Change a booking in seconds — freed capacity returns instantly
The Challenge
"Plans shift. Meetings move, children get sick, the train is cancelled. How does someone cancel, shorten, swap, or hand over a booking in seconds — and how does the freed capacity get back into circulation cleanly?"
The Four Change Types
CancelDesk released immediately back to available pool
ShortenReduce to AM or PM — partial day freed for others
SwapMove to another desk, same day or future date
Hand OverTransfer to a teammate — conflict check included
Perch's Answer
  • Single-tap change flow — no confirmation dialogs, no re-booking forms. The action is immediate and reversible within a short grace window.
  • Real-time capacity release — freed desks reappear in the booking engine within seconds, visible to other users immediately
  • Conflict-aware hand-over — before transferring, the system checks the recipient's existing bookings to prevent double-booking
  • No penalty system — the platform expects plans to change and treats every change as a normal, healthy behaviour, not a failure
  • Delegate booking — EAs and managers can book, change, and cancel on behalf of colleagues without needing their credentials
SQ-05  ·  Familiarity Without Fixed Desks
Make hot desking feel reliable and familiar
The Challenge
"Hot desking fails when it feels random. How might the system make it feel reliable — returning someone to a familiar desk, recognising routines, or letting teams hold a cluster across days without locking the space down?"
How scoring creates familiarity
Booked this desk 4× before+25 pts
Your neighbourhood assignment+20 pts
3 teammates booked nearby+18 pts
Same floor, same day-of-week+12 pts
Perch's Answer
  • History-weighted scoring — desks you've used frequently score higher automatically. You return to the same area without a fixed allocation.
  • Neighbourhood as home zone — every user belongs to a neighbourhood. Even in a full hot-desk setup, you always land in your team's area.
  • Team cluster logic — when teammates book the same day, adjacent desks are boosted in scoring so the team naturally clusters together
  • Day-of-week routine — if you always book a Tuesday desk on the 3rd floor, that pattern is recognised and your usual area scores highest on Tuesdays
  • Temporary cluster requests — managers can request a desk cluster for team days. The desks are soft-held without permanent allocation, freeing up on unused days.
SQ-06  ·  Shape Habits, Don't Police Them
Gentle nudges that build good habits without surveillance
The Challenge
"Most desk-booking problems are behavioural, not technical. How might the system gently shape better habits — timely reminders, social visibility, smart defaults — without policing people or stacking up policy?"
Design principle
Every nudge in Perch is helpful, not punitive. There is no attendance tracking, no compliance score visible to managers, and no penalty for any booking behaviour. The system assumes good intent.
Perch's Answer
  • Dashboard anchor day alert — an amber card shows upcoming anchor days with no booking in the next 14 days, with one-click links to book each date
  • Push notifications — a personalised reminder for each unbooked anchor day, sent 48h in advance. Dismissed in one tap, never repeated.
  • Social visibility via Who's In — seeing that five teammates are going in on Thursday is the most effective nudge of all. No system needed — just the information.
  • Smart defaults — the booking form pre-selects the best-match desk. The user confirms or adjusts. Friction is minimal, choice quality is high.
  • 5pm day-before reminder — if an anchor day is tomorrow and no desk is booked, a gentle prompt arrives at end of day with a pre-filled booking ready to confirm
SQ-07  ·  Rules That Flex
One rule rarely fits every case — rules that compose
The Challenge
"Team bookings, anchor days, all-hands, training cohorts, visitor groups — how might the rules flex for the use case in front of them without becoming restrictive for the everyday booker?"
Rule composition example
PersonalAlice's anchor: Mon, Fri
TeamPlatform Eng: Wed + Thu
SiteEdinburgh: Wed all-hands
EffectiveMon, Wed, Thu, Fri — union, no conflict
Perch's Answer
  • Composable anchor day rules — personal, team, and site-level anchor days union together. They never conflict; they add. The everyday booker sees only the result.
  • Team Bookings flow — managers request a coordinated group booking for 3+ people. Team members approve their own seat within the cluster.
  • Delegate booking — any user can be nominated as a delegate to book on behalf of others (EAs, ops teams, visiting groups)
  • All-hands day configuration — admin marks a date as a whole-site expected attendance day with one setting, triggering notifications to all users at that site
  • Cohort cluster requests — training groups or visitor cohorts can request a block of adjacent desks for a date range, held without permanent allocation
